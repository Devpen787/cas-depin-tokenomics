Analytical Framework
This analysis documents my journey in dissecting the DePIN landscape. My
goal is to answer one fundamental question: Which tokenomic model best
fits a specific type of business?
Through analyzing various projects, I have categorized the current landscape
into three core frameworks. I use this classification to assess whether a
project’s incentive structure aligns with its operational reality. So let’s begin
with the widely adopted one.
1. Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME)
Introduction
The Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME) is a widely adopted tokenomic
model within the DePIN sector. This mechanism operates on a dual-asset
structure: a tradable native utility token and a non-transferable credit (often
pegged to a fiat currency, like USD). While the native token is used to
incentivize miners, the credit token serves as a stable unit of account,
ensuring that service costs remain predictable regardless of the native
token’s price volatility.
Here is how it works: Users pay for services using the native utility token.

Core Logic
The fundamental principle is that users must burn the native utility token in
exchange for services. In this sense, the native token functions as the “gas
fee” or fuel for the network.
Two Different Variants
I. Classic BME
In a typical Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium, 100% of the native utility tokens
paid are burned upon service processing. Technically, these tokens are not
erased from the ledger but are sent to a “burn address” where they can
never be accessed again. This action permanently reduces the circulating
supply, leading to deflationary effects and value accrual. Consequently, the
potential for token price appreciation incentivizes miners and node
operators to maintain and expand the network.
Prominent examples include Render Network and Helium.
While the traditional BME model appears robust in theory, it faces two
critical underlying issues, particularly during the network’s nascent and
mature stages:
Early Stage Oversupply (Inflation risk): During the bootstrapping phase,

Late Stage Supply Depletion (Deflation risk): Since continuous burning is

In a system with a hard supply cap, the tokens could eventually be
depleted. This scarcity would make it impossible to reward nodes,
inevitably halting the project. To address this sustainability paradox, an
evolved mechanism known as Net Emission was introduced.
II. Net Emission
Unlike the traditional BME model, Net Emissions introduces a recycling
mechanism. Instead of permanently removing 100% of the burned tokens
from the ecosystem, the protocol “re-mints” (or recycles) a portion of the
burned tokens to reward node operators and miners. This ensures that even
when the supply cap is reached, there is a continuous reservoir of tokens
available for incentives, effectively underpinning the project’s long-term
sustainability.
The core difference between Classic BME (asset-heavy) and Net Emissions
(labor/data-heavy) lies in the profile of the supplier.
Classic BME (Asset-Heavy): Suppliers typically invest in expensive
hardware (e.g., high-end GPUs or connectivity devices). To justify these
upfront costs (Capex) and ongoing utility fees, the network must
prioritize token price appreciation. High volatility is acceptable, or even
desired, to shorten the payback period.

2. Buyback-and-Burn
The “Buyback-and-Burn” mechanism shares similarities with the Burn-and-
Mint Equilibrium (BME) model, as both aim to accrue value to the token. The
key distinction, however, lies in the source of the tokens destined for
burning. In a typical BME model, tokens are burned by users (or protocol
layers) to access services. In contrast, the Buy-and-Burn mechanism involves
the project allocating a portion of its fiat or stablecoin revenue to purchase
utility tokens from the open market, which are then permanently removed
from circulation.
Why do some networks opt for Buy-and-Burn over BME? In my view, market
sentiment and customer profile are the deciding factors.
From a market sentiment perspective, utilizing revenue for open-market
buybacks creates direct buying pressure. This functions similarly to stock
buybacks in traditional finance — a bullish signal for investors indicating
asset appreciation potential. This positive feedback loop benefits not just
holders, but also node operators.

Buyback-and-Distribute (often referred to as the “Real-Yield” model)
emerges as a compelling variant. In this mechanism, the protocol purchases
tokens from the open market but, instead of burning them, distributes them
to a staking pool.
This approach offers dual benefits: it reduces sell pressure, thereby
supporting token appreciation, while simultaneously achieving “token
recycling.” Unlike burning, which permanently sending the assets to
uncoverable address, recycling ensures a sustainable inventory of tokens to
incentivize future contributions without relying solely on inflation.
Projects like Aethir prioritize this recycling model over a permanent burn
mechanism because their primary goal is sustained infrastructure scaling
and long-term ecosystem health, rather than creating artificial scarcity for
short-term price impact.

the consensus, while penalizing dishonest or inaccurate validators.
Proponent of this model believe that token value will be sustained by the
quality of intelligence produced and the organic demand to stake and access
these services, rather than by artifically manipulating supply.

Closing Thoughts: The Evolution of Value
In my view, the most effective way to design tokenomics is to deconstruct the
business model down to its fundamental supply and demand drivers, while
benchmarking against successful Web 2 counterparts. This “back-to-basics”
approach allows us to extract the most fitting economic structures for Web 3.
Furthermore, I’ve observed the tokenomic isn’t static. As the DePIN
landscape matures, we may see protocols evolve: shifting from stability-
focused models like BME during the bootstrapping phase, to value-capture
models like Buyback and burn once cash flows stabilize.
