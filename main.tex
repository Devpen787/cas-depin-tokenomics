\section*{Abstract}
Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks (DePIN) use token-based incentives to bootstrap and sustain real-world infrastructure operated by independent providers. While tokenomics is often discussed through growth and valuation narratives, less attention is given to how mechanisms behave under adverse conditions such as demand contraction, liquidity dislocations, or deteriorating provider economics \cite{Messari2024, FrontiersDePIN2025}. This matters in DePIN because physical deployment constraints and hardware sunk costs can make participation loss slow to reverse and service-capacity deterioration persistent.

This thesis evaluates DePIN tokenomic robustness under standardized stress inputs using DTSE (DePIN Tokenomic Stress Evaluator), a reproducible simulation-based framework for comparative mechanism analysis. The objective is not to forecast token prices or project real-world outcomes, but to compare directional stress behavior across incentive structures under common assumptions and scenario controls \cite{Morris2019}. DTSE is implemented as an agent-based model in which heterogeneous infrastructure providers interact with rule-based protocol dynamics under exogenous demand and macroeconomic regimes \cite{Braakman2022}.

The framework is applied to the Onocoy network and ONO token as the primary empirical case anchor. Results are interpreted as model-conditional evidence about mechanism resilience within the defined scenario grid, not as causal or financial claims about live markets. The core contribution is a structured, auditable methodology for stress-oriented comparison of DePIN tokenomics, together with an operational vocabulary for diagnosing failure-mode signatures under explicit modeling assumptions.
