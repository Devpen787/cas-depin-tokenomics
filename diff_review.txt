-\subsubsection{The "Disease": Defining Stress Factors in Physical Networks}
-In the context of this static analysis, stress factors are exogenous shocks that challenge the economic viability of the network. We categorize these stressors into three primary buckets that determine long-term sustainability \cite{RapidInnovation2024}:
+\subsubsection{Defining Stress Factors in Physical Networks}
+In this static framework, stress factors are exogenous shocks that challenge economic viability. Three stress families are used \cite{Lin2024, Ho2022}:
 
 \begin{itemize}
-    \item \textbf{The Subsidy Gap (Reward Addiction):} This represents the structural deficit between the real operating costs (OpEx) plus capital expenditures (CapEx) of infrastructure providers and the actual fiat revenue entering the system. In early-stage networks, this gap is bridged by speculative token incentives. A critical stress point occurs when token prices decline, widening this gap and forcing providers to operate at a loss. Static analysis measures this by tracking the historical divergence between "Real Yield" (burn-based revenue) and "Dilutive Yield" (inflationary rewards) over time \cite{RapidInnovation2024}.
-    \item \textbf{Speculative Fragility:} This metric defines the correlation between network security (provider uptime/retention) and token price volatility. High fragility implies that a drop in token price triggers immediate and proportional infrastructure churn. In a non-simulated framework, this is measured by calculating the "Beta" of active node counts relative to the token's price drawdown during specific bear market windows (e.g., the 2022-2023 crypto winter) \cite{RapidInnovation2024}.
-    \item \textbf{Competitive Yield Pressure:} This refers to the elasticity of provider participation when alternative networks offer higher returns for similar hardware. As DePIN hardware becomes more generalized (e.g., GPUs for Render vs. io.net, or GNSS receivers for Geodnet vs. Onocoy), the switching costs decrease, increasing the risk of "vampire attacks" where supply migrates instantly to the highest bidder \cite{RapidInnovation2024}.
+    \item \textbf{The Subsidy Gap (Reward Addiction):} the gap between provider CapEx/OpEx and usage-linked fiat value entering the network. In early phases this gap is often bridged by token incentives; when token value compresses, the gap can widen \cite{Lin2024, McConaghy2020}. In DePIN terms, this asks whether usage-linked value can fund participation without persistent subsidy.
+    \item \textbf{Speculative Fragility:} the sensitivity of participation proxies (uptime/retention/activity) to token-price volatility. High fragility means participation degrades quickly when token-denominated rewards lose fiat value \cite{Ho2022}. In DePIN terms, this asks how tightly hardware participation tracks token price stress.
+    \item \textbf{Competitive Yield Pressure:} participation elasticity when comparable external opportunities offer higher returns for similar hardware profiles \cite{Lin2024, Chiu2024}. In DePIN terms, this captures whether operators can reallocate capacity when adjacent networks offer better reward conditions.
 \end{itemize}

+\subsubsection{Mechanism Design as a Static Defense}
+Within a static framework, mechanism design is evaluated by its ability to absorb stress:
 
 \begin{itemize}
-    \item \textbf{Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME):} Utilized by Helium and Render, this dual-token model requires users to burn the native token to create non-transferable data credits (fixed in fiat value). -    \item \textbf{Sunk Cost Moats:} A crucial differentiator for DePIN is that the hardware itself often acts as a "staked" asset. +    \item \textbf{Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME):} in common DePIN implementations, users burn token-linked credits while provider rewards are distributed via issuance channels \cite{RenderBME}. A key static check is whether usage-linked burn can keep pace with reward outflow in adverse windows.
+    \item \textbf{Capped Supply with Programmed Distribution:} for Onocoy, publicly documented mechanics indicate a capped/pre-minted supply with rule-based distribution and buyback-burn routing, rather than uncapped new creation \cite{OnocoyWhitepaper301, OnocoyToken, OnocoyRewards, OnocoyBonus}. +    \item \textbf{Sunk Cost Moats:} physical hardware is often illiquid and location-bound, creating real exit friction that can delay churn during drawdowns \cite{Arthur1989, Ballandies2023}. In DePIN terms, this is a retention buffer from physical frictions, not necessarily from incentive strength.
 \end{itemize}
 
 \subsubsection{Methodology: Empirical Event Studies}
-To replace simulation, we utilize the Event Study Methodology, a standard approach in financial econometrics used to measure the impact of specific events on asset value and participant behavior \cite{Hacken2024}. By treating historical market shocks—such as the FTX collapse, Helium's migration to Solana, or Hivemapper's reward restructuring—as distinct "events," we can quantify the abnormal churn or abnormal returns associated with these stressors.
+This chapter applies event-study logic from financial econometrics \cite{MacKinlay1997}. Historical shocks (for example, broad crypto drawdowns, mechanism transitions, and reward-structure changes) are treated as event windows. Within those windows, participation and incentive metrics are compared to pre-event baselines to identify abnormal deviations in churn, utilization, and solvency proxies.
 
-This method allows us to infer the future behavior of a network like Onocoy by benchmarking it against the past empirical behavior of comparable networks under similar conditions. We look for "Abnormal Churn Rates"—deviations from the expected baseline of provider exits—following negative price shocks to determine the true "Elasticity of Provider Exit" \cite{RapidInnovation2024}.
+This empirical layer does not estimate causal treatment effects. It provides structured comparative evidence for stress signatures that later inform DTSE scenario design and interpretation boundaries.

+\subsection{DePIN Failure Modes: Diagnostic Matrix}
+Based on the empirical cases and the stress framework, this chapter uses five operational failure-mode labels as diagnostic categories (not forecasts) \cite{Braakman2022, Morris2019}.
 
 \begin{enumerate}
-    \item \textbf{Reward-Demand Decoupling:} Emissions persist at a high rate while network usage (demand) flatlines or drops. If GNSS stations deploy faster than RTK data buyers can be onboarded, ONO rewards will dilute rapidly \cite{Coincub2025}.

-    \item \textbf{The Death Spiral:} Feedback loop where Price Drop $\to$ Lower Yield $\to$ Miner Churn $\to$ Lower Security $\to$ Lower Demand $\to$ Further Price Drop \cite{MediumHeliumMiner}.
+    \item \textbf{Reward--Demand Decoupling:} reward outflow remains high while usage-linked sink strength weakens \cite{FrontiersDePIN2025, OnocoyRewards}. In DePIN terms, rewards continue while service demand lags.
+    \item \textbf{Liquidity-Driven Incentive Compression:} market-side token dislocation reduces fiat-equivalent reward value and compresses provider economics \cite{Ho2022, Lin2024}. In DePIN terms, effective returns fall even when physical costs are stable.
+    \item \textbf{Latent Capacity Degradation:} participation appears stable while maintenance intensity and quality buffers erode \cite{Arthur1989, Ballandies2023}. In DePIN terms, reported node counts can mask declining resilience.
+    \item \textbf{Elastic Provider Exit:} operators reallocate to better external yields when hardware compatibility permits \cite{Chiu2024, Lin2024, GeodnetLocationNFT}. In DePIN terms, supply can move faster than demand.
+    \item \textbf{Dilution Feedback Loop:} weaker token value reduces effective reward, participation, and service confidence, which can further pressure value channels \cite{Lin2024, FrontiersDePIN2025}. In DePIN terms, stress channels can reinforce each other.
 \end{enumerate}

-These regimes abstract broader market sentiment and liquidity conditions without attempting to model specific macroeconomic variables. Their purpose is to expose tokenomic mechanisms to varying background conditions rather than to replicate real-world cycles.

-Stress dimensions are combined according to clearly defined rules. -\section{Evaluation Metrics}
+\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Operational Thresholds}
 \label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
+DTSE uses a pre-specified metric set defined before results analysis. Metrics are selected to be (i) comparative across mechanisms and scenarios, (ii) mechanism-relevant for provider incentives and service delivery, and (iii) transparent in computation and interpretation \cite{Morris2019}. Metrics are used to compare relative robustness under standardized stress, not to establish optimal designs or to make real-world performance claims.
 
-\subsection{Principles for Metric Selection}
-Evaluating the robustness of tokenomic mechanisms requires metrics that are both interpretable and aligned with the functional objectives of DePIN networks. Three principles guide metric selection in this thesis: metrics must be comparative, enabling evaluation of relative performance across mechanisms and scenarios rather than absolute success or failure; they must be mechanism-relevant, reflecting provider incentives, service delivery, or economic sustainability rather than speculative market outcomes; and they must be transparent, with clearly defined computation and interpretation.
-
-These principles align with established guidance for simulation-based evaluation, where metrics should illuminate system behavior without overstating precision \cite{Morris2019}.
-
-\subsection{Core Sustainability Metrics}
-The following core metrics are used to evaluate system behavior under stress.
-
-\subsubsection{Provider Retention and Churn}
-Provider retention is measured as the proportion of active providers remaining over time, while churn captures the rate and magnitude of provider exit events. These metrics directly reflect the stability of the infrastructure layer and are central to DePIN sustainability.
-
-Retention is interpreted directionally: higher retention under comparable stress indicates greater robustness, but does not imply optimal or permanent stability.
-
-\subsubsection{Capacity Utilization and Service Continuity}
-Service continuity is assessed through capacity utilization and demand satisfaction rates. Capacity utilization measures the extent to which deployed infrastructure is effectively used, while demand satisfaction captures the proportion of demand that can be served given available capacity.
-
-Together, these metrics indicate whether incentive mechanisms support not only provider participation but also functional service delivery.
-
-\subsubsection{Incentive Solvency (Burn-to-Mint Ratio)}
-Incentive solvency is proxied by the ratio of value burned through usage-driven sinks to value emitted through token issuance. A ratio approaching or exceeding unity suggests reduced reliance on subsidies, while persistent divergence indicates ongoing subsidy dependency.
+\paragraph{Controlled comparability vs.\ profile specificity}
+DTSE enforces controlled comparability by holding constant the reporting horizon, scenario definitions (the frozen grid), aggregation conventions, and the pre-specified metric vocabulary used for comparative readouts across profiles. Mechanism profiles are not made structurally identical: each profile instantiates its own mechanism rule set and may differ through profile-specific parameters and overrides, including the heterogeneity structure used to represent agent- or operator-level variation. These profile-specific settings are treated as modeled assumptions rather than empirical estimates, and are recorded for traceability in the configuration register, threshold registry, and frozen run manifest (Appendix A.3--A.7). Results are therefore interpreted as baseline-relative responses to identical stress inputs under different mechanism profiles, not as magnitude-equivalent rankings of real-world protocols.
 
-This metric abstracts complex monetary dynamics into a single comparative signal and is interpreted cautiously as an indicator rather than a definitive threshold.

+\subsection{Reproducibility, Verification, and Limitations}
+Reproducibility is treated as a core requirement. Each DTSE experiment is defined by an explicit parameterization, scenario input set, and random-seed policy, enabling regeneration and inspection without interactive tuning.

+\subsubsection*{Monte Carlo aggregation and dispersion reporting}
+Stochastic elements (demand variation, provider heterogeneity sampling, churn decision noise, and price-related noise) are evaluated through repeated simulation runs with seeded pseudo-random number generation. -\subsection{Interpretation and Limitations of Metrics}
-All metrics used in this thesis are subject to limitations arising from model assumptions and abstraction. In particular, simplified price formation and exogenous demand constrain the interpretation of monetary indicators. Accordingly, metrics are used to compare relative robustness, not to establish optimal designs or real-world predictions.
+\subsubsection*{Verification (internal validity)}
+Verification is framed as internal consistency checking under stated assumptions rather than empirical validation. Checks include baseline runs without stress (boundedness and invariants), controlled perturbations (directional response under isolated inputs), and sensitivity analysis over key parameter families (cost ranges, emission limits, churn thresholds).
+\cite{Collins2024, Braakman2022}.
 
-This cautious interpretation aligns with best practices in simulation-based research and supports the thesis's evaluative rather than predictive orientation \cite{Braakman2022}.
+\subsubsection*{Limitations and interpretation boundaries}
+DTSE abstracts from several factors that may matter in practice, including endogenous user formation, strategic provider behavior beyond the modeled decision rules, governance interventions, legal constraints, and detailed market microstructure. The modeled price signal is reduced-form, and demand is exogenous by design. These choices preserve interpretability and experimental control but limit external validity. Accordingly, DTSE outputs are reported as comparative, model-conditional indicators of robustness and failure sensitivity, not as forecasts.
