\section{Theoretical Tokenomic Framework}
\label{sec:theoretical_framework}

\subsection{Introduction}
This chapter defines the mechanism-level logic used to compare DePIN tokenomic designs under stress. The focus is not short-term token performance, but structural relationships between infrastructure demand, incentive issuance, value-capture pathways, and provider retention constraints in hardware-dependent networks.
\cite{Ballandies2023, Lin2024}.

The analytical lens is comparative: protocols are interpreted as design bundles that combine (i) emission logic, (ii) work verification, and (iii) monetization/value-accrual pathways. This provides a common basis for stress-oriented comparison before empirical analysis and DTSE parameterization.
\cite{Morris2019, Braakman2022}.

\subsection{DePIN as a Cyber-Physical System}
\label{subsec:depin_cps}
This research defines Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks (DePIN), and specifically the Onocoy network, not merely as cryptocurrency projects, but as Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS). In this framing, physical hardware (GNSS reference stations) and digital state (token incentives) are treated as a coupled feedback system.

The CPS framing in this chapter uses three working propositions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Coupled loop logic:} hardware state and incentive state co-evolve rather than operating as separable layers.
    \item \textbf{Token as coordination signal:} the token operates as what Voshmgir describes as a ``Purpose-Driven Token'' for collective infrastructure deployment \cite{Voshmgir2020}.
    \item \textbf{Engineering orientation:} the analysis follows Token Engineering traditions rooted in CPS design rather than speculative-finance narratives.
\end{itemize}

BlockScience, founded by Zargham, has established the industry standard for modeling complex cryptoeconomic systems, having successfully applied these principles to the Ethereum gas market, The Graph, and most notably, Filecoin.
\cite{Bernardineli2022, McConaghy2020}.
Their work on Filecoin is particularly relevant as a comparative baseline for Onocoy, given the shared challenge of incentivizing massive-scale physical infrastructure deployment.

Aligning with this industry standard, this thesis adopts a ``Digital Twin'' simulation methodology centered on DTSE (DePIN Tokenomic Stress Evaluator). The modeling choice is used in three explicit steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reference anchor:} BlockScience's cadCAD-style cryptoeconomic simulation practice is treated as a methodological precedent for CPS-oriented mechanism analysis.
    \item \textbf{DTSE role:} this thesis uses a specialized low-fidelity digital twin to model feedback loops among hardware CAPEX, provider retention, and token-side variance rather than to forecast token prices.
    \item \textbf{Analytical objective:} control-theoretic simulation is used to surface failure-mode signatures in systems where physical infrastructure creates delayed adjustment dynamics \cite{Bernardineli2022}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparative Scope: The Solana DePIN Ecosystem}
\label{subsec:solana_scope}
The comparative scope follows the contributor submission baseline: a sample centered on large Solana-native or Solana-bridged DePIN protocols with verifiable infrastructure services (wireless, GNSS correction, mapping, compute, or data infrastructure). The practical objective is not exhaustive market coverage, but a representative mechanism set for stress comparison.
\cite{Messari2024, CoinMarketCapDePIN}.

\subsubsection{Project Archetypes}
The comparator set is organized by infrastructure profile and dominant incentive logic:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Helium (IoT/Mobile):} canonical burn-credit architecture and mixed IoT/mobile demand.
    \item \textbf{Hivemapper (Mapping):} active labor plus consumption-linked burn model.
    \item \textbf{Render (Compute):} GPU-heavy economics with compute-demand cyclicality.
    \item \textbf{io.net (Aggregated Compute):} multi-currency demand unit and compute-market routing.
    \item \textbf{Geodnet (GNSS):} GNSS service monetization with burn-linked value-capture claims.
    \item \textbf{Grass (Bandwidth):} high participation narrative with comparatively opaque accrual pathway.
    \item \textbf{Nosana (CI/CD/AI jobs):} native-token job settlement with weaker explicit structural sinks.
    \item \textbf{Aleph.im (Storage/Compute):} utility-token framing with limited public granularity on accrual mechanics.
\end{enumerate}
\cite{Coincub2025, GeodnetIP7, IoNet2025, CoinMarketCapHM, OnocoyToken}.

\subsection{Tokenomic Building Blocks}
\label{subsec:building_blocks}

\subsubsection{Emission Schedules \& Inflation}
Three issuance families are central to this thesis comparison:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time-based decay / halving:} supply issuance falls according to a predefined schedule, independent of short-term demand.
    \item \textbf{Linear decay variants:} issuance decreases progressively to reduce long-run dilution pressure.
    \item \textbf{State-dependent minting:} issuance is conditioned on measurable network state (e.g., capacity growth), slowing inflation when network growth stalls.
\end{itemize}

Traditional cryptocurrencies often rely on rigid, time-based emission schedules, such as Bitcoin's ``Halving'' model, which reduces supply regardless of demand or network health. However, recent infrastructure protocols have moved toward ``Linear Decay'' or ``State-Dependent'' issuance. Most notably, Filecoin's concept of ``Effective Network Time'' advances issuance only as physical capacity is added to the network \cite{Bernardineli2022}. This creates a feedback mechanism where the protocol slows its inflation during periods of stagnation, preserving value for future growth, a critical design pattern for capital-intensive DePIN projects.

For stress analysis, the practical implication is straightforward:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed time schedules} can continue distribution under weak demand.
    \item \textbf{State-dependent schedules} can damp inflation when growth stalls.
    \item \textbf{Design choice} changes how quickly subsidy pressure appears under adverse regimes.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME)}
BME describes a closed-loop structure where service usage creates an explicit sink (typically burn-linked through utility credits), while provider incentives are funded via controlled issuance. In practice, BME implementations differ in strictness: some systems use hard burn-credit coupling, while others combine burns with treasury routing, staking locks, or variable fee splits.
\cite{RenderBME, GeodnetIP7, CoinMarketCapHM}.

This structure aligns with the ``Web3 Sustainability Loop'' proposed by Trent McConaghy (Ocean Protocol), which posits that sustainable data economies require a closed-loop value flow where network revenue continuously offsets token emissions \cite{McConaghy2020}.

\subsubsection{Work Verification Mechanisms}
DePIN verification design determines who is paid, for what action, and under which quality constraints. Representative mechanisms include proof-of-coverage style attestations (wireless), job-completion proofs (compute), and outcome-quality validation (e.g., usable mapping or GNSS correction contribution). The mechanism choice directly affects reward targeting, fraud surface, and robustness under stress.
\cite{Ballandies2023, Chiu2024}.

Following the taxonomy of token incentives defined by Lisa JY Tan (Economics Design), Onocoy's mechanism relies on ``outcome-based'' rewards (verified data) rather than ``action-based'' rewards, distinguishing it from simpler staking protocols \cite{Tan2020}.

\subsection{Monetization and Demand Regimes}
\label{subsec:demand_regimes}

\subsubsection{The Value Capture Funnel}
The core value-capture pathway can be formalized as:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{Fiat/User Payment} &\rightarrow \text{Protocol Settlement Layer} \\
&\rightarrow \text{Token Sink or Buy Pressure} \\
&\rightarrow \text{Net Supply Pressure}
\end{aligned}
\]

In the contributor baseline submission, comparator projects clustered into three monetization architectures: (i) fiat-front-end with tokenized backend settlement and burn-linked sinks, (ii) native-token-first payment rails, and (iii) accrual-opaque designs with insufficient public documentation for robust quantification.
\cite{Ballandies2023, Lin2024}.

\subsubsection{Demand Cyclicality: Enterprise vs. Consumer}
Demand regime structure differs materially across use cases. Enterprise or professional demand (e.g., GNSS correction, production compute workloads) tends to be more contract-like and potentially less reflexive than retail/consumer demand cycles. Consumer-heavy networks can scale quickly but are typically more narrative-sensitive and prone to sharper usage swings. This distinction matters because the same emission and sink design can behave differently under B2B-stable versus B2C-cyclical demand.
\cite{Lin2024, Ho2022}.

\subsection{Detailed Analysis of the ONO Mechanism}
\label{subsec:ono_mechanism}
Onocoy is modeled as a capped-supply design with deflationary elements, rather than a pure unlimited-mint BME. The architecture combines ONO (transferable utility/governance token) with fiat-denominated, non-transferable Data Credits consumed on usage. This attempts to separate user-facing pricing stability from token volatility while preserving a demand-coupled sink path through service consumption.
\cite{OnocoyWhitepaper301, OnocoyToken}.

Compared with pure BME variants, this design shifts stress exposure: it can reduce user-price friction under volatile token markets, but its long-run resilience depends on whether demand-side cashflows and sink intensity are sufficient to offset issuance and maintain provider economics as emissions decay.
\cite{OnocoyWhitepaper301, Braakman2022}.

\paragraph*{Audit Lens Used for ONO}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{User-side stability question:} does the Data-Credit layer reduce short-horizon token-volatility transmission to service pricing?
    \item \textbf{Provider-economics question:} do sink strength and reward-distribution dynamics remain aligned as emissions normalize?
    \item \textbf{Comparative role:} ONO is assessed with the same stress vocabulary used for BME comparators to preserve cross-profile interpretability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Conclusion}
The theoretical comparison yields three vulnerability classes that motivate the empirical and simulation phases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reward--Demand Decoupling:} emissions persist while realized service demand weakens.
    \item \textbf{Accrual Ambiguity:} weak public transparency on sink paths prevents robust solvency inference.
    \item \textbf{Demand-Regime Mismatch:} monetization architecture and issuance logic calibrated for one demand type fail under another.
\end{itemize}

These vulnerabilities provide the bridge to empirical stress analysis and DTSE scenario design, where mechanism performance is evaluated under standardized adverse regimes rather than inferred from narrative claims.
