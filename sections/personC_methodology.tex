\section{Methodology: Simulation-Based Stress Testing}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Purpose, Scope, and Context-of-Use}
This chapter introduces DTSE (DePIN Tokenomic Stress Evaluator), a simulation-based framework used to compare how DePIN tokenomic mechanisms behave under the same adverse inputs. The purpose is comparative: DTSE is used to evaluate relative sensitivity, trade-offs, and failure-mode signatures across mechanisms exposed to identical stress conditions. Results are interpreted as model-conditional comparisons rather than absolute performance scores or real-world projections \cite{Morris2019, Braakman2022}.

The thesis requires an evaluator that can bridge mechanism design (e.g., Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium versus capped-supply structures) to stress behavior in hardware-dependent networks. Treating Onocoy as a cyber-physical system (Section~\ref{subsec:depin_cps}), DTSE is used to represent feedback between protocol incentive rules, provider participation decisions, and service capacity under exogenously specified stress regimes.

Several non-goals bound interpretation. DTSE does not generate token price forecasts, valuation estimates, or market-capitalization projections. Price dynamics appear only as internal state variables driven by explicitly modeled assumptions (Section~\ref{subsec:dtse_model_overview_assumptions}). Outputs are not treated as causal claims about real-world networks. Patterns reported in Section~\ref{sec:simulation_results} remain conditional on model structure, parameterization, and stress specification.

DTSE does not identify optimal parameter settings and does not prescribe tokenomic designs. Its role is to provide a transparent and reproducible basis for mechanism-level comparison under stress while keeping assumptions explicit and auditable \cite{Morris2019}.

\subsection{DTSE Model Overview and Assumptions}
\label{subsec:dtse_model_overview_assumptions}
DTSE is implemented as an agent-based simulation where heterogeneous infrastructure providers interact with a rule-based protocol under exogenous stress inputs \cite{Collins2024, McCulloch2022}. The modeling choice is practical: it captures provider heterogeneity and threshold behavior without requiring a full market microstructure model.

\subsubsection*{Separation of fact, modeled assumption, and interpretation boundary}
DTSE distinguishes three evidentiary layers that are used consistently throughout the thesis.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Mechanism facts:} protocol-defined rules (e.g., issuance, burns/sinks, and reward allocation logic) sourced from official documentation and instantiated as deterministic rule sets in the simulator.
    \item \emph{Modeled assumptions:} abstractions required for experimental control (e.g., exogenous demand regimes, reduced-form price-process signals, and provider cost distributions).
    \item \emph{Interpretation boundaries:} what DTSE outputs mean, namely comparative indicators under stated assumptions rather than empirical measurements or claims of external validity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Agents and decision rules}
Providers are modeled as heterogeneous agents with differentiated cost structures and effective capacity contributions. Behavior is restricted to economically motivated decisions (entry, continuation, exit/churn) based on modeled profitability thresholds and accumulated losses \cite{Collins2024, McCulloch2022}.

Users are not modeled as individual agents. Instead, service demand is specified as an exogenous input (Section~\ref{sec:stress_scenarios}) to support controlled comparison across mechanisms \cite{Morris2019, Braakman2022}.

\subsubsection*{Protocol abstraction and endogenous state}
The protocol is represented as a rule-based system governing emissions, reward allocation, usage-linked burns/sinks, and internal price-process dynamics. Given a parameterization and exogenous inputs, protocol behavior is deterministic and does not adapt strategically within runs (e.g., no governance intervention). Endogenous state variables include token supply, emissions and burns, provider profitability, churn/retention, utilization/capacity, and incentive-solvency proxies.

\subsubsection*{Price-process signal (model scope)}
The modeled token price series is a reduced-form internal signal that responds to specified assumptions about demand, liquidity depth, and supply-side dynamics. It is included to translate token-denominated incentives into in-model provider revenue and margin dynamics, not to reproduce market microstructure or to forecast real prices.
\cite{Morris2019, Collins2024}.

\subsubsection*{Theory-to-parameter translation}
DTSE operationalizes the thesis stress constructs by mapping them to explicit parameter families used in stress experiments. Reward Addiction is evaluated using burn-to-mint and emission-cap related parameters; Subsidy Gap is evaluated through provider-cost assumptions and usage-linked fiat-revenue baselines; Speculative Fragility is evaluated through churn sensitivity under adverse modeled price conditions.
The authoritative mapping table is provided in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_theory_mapping}.

\subsubsection*{Parameterization discipline}
Parameters are treated as \emph{ranges and regimes} rather than point estimates. Where practitioner context or public documentation informs plausible bounds, it is used only to select broad intervals for sensitivity analysis and is not treated as empirical evidence for mechanism claims.
The parameter and distribution register is documented in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_config_register}.

\subsection{Stress Scenario Specification (DTSE-Selected Stylized Channels)}
\label{sec:stress_scenarios}
In this thesis, stress is defined as an externally imposed adverse condition applied as a DTSE input. Stress is not defined as an outcome (e.g., price decline or provider exit), which avoids circularity. Stress scenarios are designed to challenge three sustainability dimensions under matched experimental conditions: provider retention, service continuity, and incentive solvency.

All stress dimensions are specified as exogenous inputs so that the same stress conditions can be applied across alternative mechanism profiles and differences in outcomes can be attributed to mechanism responses under the DTSE assumptions \cite{Morris2019}.

\subsubsection*{Macroeconomic regimes (stylized background conditions)}
DTSE specifies regime-based modifiers that influence the modeled price-process drift and volatility (bearish, sideways, bullish). These regimes are DTSE-selected stylizations used to vary background conditions for comparative evaluation.

\subsubsection*{Demand regimes (exogenous service usage)}
Demand is modeled as an exogenous stochastic time series with multiple stylized forms (stable, growth, volatile, high-to-decay). Demand remains exogenous by design to isolate provider-side incentive responses under identical demand trajectories.

\subsubsection*{Liquidity shocks (discrete dislocations)}
Liquidity stress is introduced through discrete unlock-style events combined with finite market depth, producing abrupt changes in the modeled price signal. The objective is to evaluate mechanism sensitivity to sudden price dislocations under specified assumptions, not to model investor behavior.

\subsubsection*{Provider economics stress (margin compression and opportunity cost)}
Provider-side stress is represented via scenario controls that shift modeled operating costs, competitor-yield opportunity parameters, and profitability thresholds. DTSE may distinguish provider types (e.g., higher-cost versus lower-cost configurations) to represent heterogeneous sensitivity to the same stress input.

\subsubsection*{Tokenomics parameter stress (design sensitivity)}
Stress is also applied directly to mechanism parameters (e.g., emission caps, burn fractions, and initial supply conditions) to evaluate how DTSE outcomes vary across design choices under standardized external stress.

\subsubsection*{Selected Chapter-8 Scenario Set and Selection Rationale}
For Chapter~\ref{sec:simulation_results}, this thesis fixes one baseline scenario and four stress scenarios: demand contraction, liquidity shock, competitive yield pressure, and provider cost inflation. The selection follows the theory-to-parameter contract in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_theory_mapping}. Each scenario is mapped to one dominant stress channel to keep interpretation traceable: demand contraction (reward--demand decoupling), liquidity shock (token-liquidity transmission), competitive yield pressure (external-opportunity churn), and provider cost inflation (Subsidy-Gap pressure).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demand contraction} tests whether usage-linked channels weaken before participation channels.
    \item \textbf{Liquidity shock} tests short-latency transmission from token-market dislocation into provider economics.
    \item \textbf{Competitive yield pressure} tests participation elasticity to outside opportunities under stable internal demand.
    \item \textbf{Provider cost inflation} tests margin compression and lagged participation response under exogenous OpEx pressure.
\end{itemize}

To keep interpretation auditable, each scenario is documented with explicit frozen inputs, a defined update rule, and baseline-relative signal logic.

Demand contraction uses a high-to-decay demand regime with \(B_{\mathrm{DC}}=10000\), \(\sigma_{\mathrm{DC}}=0.08\), and baseline reference \(B_{\mathrm{BL}}=12000\), \(\sigma_{\mathrm{BL}}=0\).

Liquidity shock applies a discrete unlock-style sell event at week \(t^*=20\) with \(\phi=0.35\), then propagates it through finite-depth AMM-style price updates.

Competitive yield pressure sets external-yield scalar \(y=1.5\) and applies type-specific provider-switching pressure (higher for Provider Type A, lower for Provider Type B) with bounded noise.

Provider cost inflation applies base-cost multiplier \(1.35\) and propagates the shift through the heterogeneous provider-cost rule used by the simulation engine.

Signal timing in Chapter~\ref{sec:simulation_results} is computed as first threshold crossing versus the ONO baseline, using frozen thresholds from Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_threshold_table}. Exact scenario formulas and timing equations are documented in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_scenario_formula_map}.

\subsubsection*{Interview-Informed Context and Assumption Bounds}
Interview-derived practitioner context is used only to inform assumption ranges and operational frictions that may be under-specified in public protocol material. In this methodology, interview signals are treated as indicative rather than deterministic, and they are never promoted to mechanism facts. No confidential internal data is published; interview-informed inputs are introduced only as bounded modeled assumptions with explicit disclosure in the configuration register.

In scope for this thesis, interview-informed signals include provider payback expectations, observed churn drivers, internal demand indicators, and practitioner views on emission-adjustment trade-offs. These signals are used for calibration ranges and stress plausibility checks, not for causal claims or standalone empirical inference.

The interpretation boundary for interview-informed material is explicit:
\begin{itemize}
    \item granular provider-level financial data is not fully public,
    \item token-market behavior may diverge from stylized model assumptions,
    \item interview responses are context-bound and potentially subjective,
    \item interview inputs are not treated as direct evidence of causal mechanisms.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Scenario combination rules}
Stress dimensions are combined according to pre-defined rules to preserve interpretability. Parameter-based stresses (macro regime, demand regime, provider economics) may be combined to form compound stress scenarios. Scenario-specific modes, if present, are treated as mutually exclusive to reduce confounding.
\cite{Morris2019, Braakman2022}.

\subsubsection*{Documentation of scenario grids}
The full scenario grid (input values, combinations, and run identifiers) is appendix-grade material and is documented in a configuration register to support auditability and reproducibility (Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_scenario_grid}). A compact rationale-to-formula audit map is provided in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_scenario_formula_map}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Operational Thresholds}
\label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
DTSE uses a pre-specified metric set defined before results analysis. Metrics are selected to be (i) comparable across mechanisms and scenarios, (ii) relevant to provider incentives and service delivery, and (iii) transparent in computation and interpretation \cite{Morris2019}. Metrics are used to compare relative robustness under standardized stress, not to establish optimal designs or to make real-world performance claims.

\paragraph{Controlled comparability vs.\ profile specificity}
DTSE enforces controlled comparability by holding constant the reporting horizon, scenario definitions (frozen grid), aggregation conventions, and pre-specified metric vocabulary. Mechanism profiles are not forced to be structurally identical: each profile instantiates its own rule set and may differ through profile-specific parameters and overrides, including heterogeneity settings for agent/operator variation. These profile-specific settings are treated as modeled assumptions rather than empirical estimates, and are documented for traceability in the configuration register, threshold registry, and frozen run manifest (Appendix A.3--A.7). Results are interpreted as baseline-relative responses to identical stress inputs under different mechanism profiles, not as magnitude-equivalent rankings of real-world protocols.

The empirical chapter uses an additional applicability gate for historical windows (Section~\ref{subsec:metric_applicability_framework}) and can mark selected metrics as \emph{N/R}. That empirical \emph{N/R} policy is a retrospective data-observability control only. It does not change DTSE metric generation in Chapter~\ref{sec:simulation_results}, where the full pre-specified metric set is produced under controlled simulation assumptions.

\subsubsection*{Core DTSE reporting equations}
Chapter~\ref{sec:simulation_results} relies on a small equation set to keep metric interpretation auditable while avoiding unnecessary formal overhead.
\begin{align*}
\text{Burn-to-Mint}_t &= \frac{\text{Burns}_t}{\max(\text{Emissions}_t,\epsilon)}, \\
\text{Net Emissions}_t &= \text{Emissions}_t - \text{Burns}_t, \\
\text{Velocity Proxy}_t &= \frac{\text{Transaction Turnover}_t}{\max(\text{Effective Token Stock}_t,\epsilon)}, \\
t_m^* &= \min \left\{ t : \frac{\left|m_t^{\mathrm{SC}}-m_t^{\mathrm{BL}}\right|}{\max\!\left(\left|m_t^{\mathrm{BL}}\right|,\epsilon\right)} \ge \theta_m \right\}, \\
\text{Incentive Efficiency} &= \frac{\sum_t (\text{Emissions}_t \times \text{Price}_t)}{\sum_t \text{Validated Capacity}_t}.
\end{align*}
These equations provide the operational definitions for solvency, emission pressure, activity intensity, baseline-relative signal timing, and capital efficiency. Scenario-specific mechanism equations remain in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_scenario_formula_map}.

\subsubsection*{Provider retention and churn}
Retention is measured as the proportion of active providers remaining over time. Churn captures the rate and magnitude of provider exit events. Retention is interpreted directionally under comparable stress and does not imply permanent stability.

\subsubsection*{Capacity utilization and service continuity}
Service continuity is assessed through utilization and demand-satisfaction rates: utilization reflects effective use of deployed capacity, while demand satisfaction reflects the proportion of modeled demand served given available capacity.

\subsubsection*{Incentive solvency (burn-to-mint ratio)}
Incentive solvency is proxied by the burn-to-mint ratio defined above. Persistent divergence is interpreted as subsidy dependence within the DTSE assumptions, not as a universal threshold claim.

\subsubsection*{Net emissions and inflation dynamics}
Net emissions follow the accounting identity defined above and track the balance between issuance and destruction over time. Changes under stress are interpreted as mechanism response signals rather than as performance targets.

\subsubsection*{Token velocity (proxy)}
Token velocity is estimated using the turnover-normalized proxy defined above. Given the abstraction of market structure, this metric is used comparatively and interpreted cautiously.
\cite{CFA2018}.

\subsubsection*{Incentive efficiency (capital efficiency)}
Incentive efficiency quantifies the modeled emission cost of validated capacity using the definition above. Lower cost-per-unit is interpreted as higher in-model efficiency under identical stress inputs, not as a direct claim about real-world competitiveness.

\subsubsection*{Volatility proxy}
Price volatility is measured using dispersion metrics derived from the modeled price series. Volatility is treated as a stress signal affecting provider incentives rather than as a market-performance indicator.

\subsubsection*{Derived summary indicators}
Two summary indicators support synthesis. (i) \emph{Death spiral probability} is defined operationally as the frequency with which simulations exhibit concurrent degradation in modeled price, provider count, and service capacity beyond pre-specified thresholds within the simulation horizon. (ii) Aggregate network revenue and average provider profitability are tracked to contextualize retention and churn dynamics \cite{Morris2019}. Threshold definitions used in this thesis are documented in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_threshold_table}.

\subsection{Reproducibility, Verification, and Limitations}
Reproducibility is treated as a core requirement. Each DTSE experiment is defined by an explicit parameterization, scenario input set, and random-seed policy, enabling regeneration and inspection without interactive tuning.
The run manifest and identifier policy are documented in Appendix Section~\ref{app:dtse_run_manifest}.

\subsubsection*{Monte Carlo aggregation and dispersion reporting}
Stochastic elements (demand variation, provider heterogeneity sampling, churn decision noise, and price-related noise) are evaluated through repeated simulation runs with seeded pseudo-random number generation. Results are summarized using distributional statistics (e.g., median and interquartile ranges) rather than relying on single trajectories \cite{Morris2019}.

\subsubsection*{Verification (internal validity)}
Verification is framed as internal consistency checking under stated assumptions rather than empirical validation. Checks include baseline runs without stress (boundedness and invariants), controlled perturbations (directional response under isolated inputs), and sensitivity analysis over key parameter families (cost ranges, emission limits, churn thresholds).
\cite{Collins2024, Braakman2022}.

\subsubsection*{Limitations and interpretation boundaries}
DTSE abstracts from several factors that may matter in practice, including endogenous user formation, strategic provider behavior beyond the modeled decision rules, governance interventions, legal constraints, and detailed market microstructure. The modeled price signal is reduced-form, and demand is exogenous by design. These choices preserve interpretability and experimental control but limit external validity. Accordingly, DTSE outputs are reported as comparative, model-conditional indicators of robustness and failure sensitivity, not as real-world projections.
