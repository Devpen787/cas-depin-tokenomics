## DTSE Option B Defense Memo

Status: synthesis control document  
Date captured: 2026-03-01  
Mode: `MODE: SYNTHESIS`

### Purpose

This memo records the agreed DTSE posture for V2 under **Option B**:

- keep DTSE,
- narrow the claims,
- defend it as a bounded comparative evaluator,
- and reduce exposure to overclaiming or pseudo-predictive language.

### Core Position

DTSE remains in the thesis because it is the mechanism that lets the thesis answer:

> how robustness can be evaluated before catastrophic failure becomes operationally visible.

But DTSE should be defended as:
- rule-based,
- transparent,
- bounded,
- reproducible,
- comparative,
- and explicitly non-predictive.

### Safe DTSE Defense Language

Use wording like:

- `DTSE is a rule-based comparative stress evaluator.`
- `It tests how different tokenomic rule sets respond to matched stress inputs under explicit assumptions.`
- `Its purpose is comparative diagnosis, not forecasting.`
- `Outputs are baseline-relative and scenario-conditional.`
- `The model uses explicit formulas, fixed scenario definitions, deterministic seeds, and repeated runs.`
- `Provider behavior is represented through transparent economic decision rules, not rich behavioral psychology.`
- `The value of DTSE is showing where stress appears first, how it propagates, and which subsystems are most sensitive under the stated contract.`

Short oral version:

> DTSE is a rule-based comparative stress evaluator with explicit formulas, frozen scenario inputs, and reproducible repeated runs. It diagnoses transmission paths under stated assumptions; it does not forecast real networks.

### Unsafe Phrases to Avoid

Do not use:

- `the simulation predicts`
- `the model shows what will happen`
- `this proves real-world superiority`
- `DTSE ranks live networks`
- `the price process reproduces the market`
- `Burn-to-Mint below 1 proves insolvency`
- `operators churn after three consecutive negative-profit weeks`

Important nuance:
- the `three consecutive weeks below zero` language is safest as a **failure-signature criterion**, not a full description of the underlying provider decision engine

### Why Option B Is the Right Posture

1. **Artifact integrity is stronger than rhetorical confidence**
   - frozen manifests, threshold registry, and export checks are already in place
   - the math/export side is not the main weakness

2. **The main risk is interpretation scope**
   - overclaiming what the outputs mean
   - sounding predictive
   - implying market realism the evaluator does not claim

3. **Option C would force a larger thesis pivot**
   - without DTSE, the thesis loses a major part of how it answers the main question

### Keep / Demote / Cut Guidance for Later Chapters

#### Keep confidently

- non-forecasting methodological contract
- fixed scenario grid
- deterministic seed schedule
- 60-run aggregation
- baseline-relative logic
- Burn-to-Mint Ratio
- Net Emissions
- Provider Retention / Churn
- Average Provider Profitability
- Capacity Utilization
- Demand Satisfaction
- Volatility Proxy
- first-signal timing logic
- failure-signature vocabulary

#### Keep, but phrase carefully

- cross-profile comparisons
- liquidity-shock transmission
- competitive-yield logic
- Onocoy-specific interpretive mapping

Rule:
- keep these directional, scenario-conditional, and model-bounded

#### Demote or trim

- broad `death spiral probability` rhetoric
- any scalar robustness score
- any language that sounds like network ranking
- any result that is harder to explain than to display

#### Cut if maximum safety is needed

- composite indicators with long defense burden
- passages that sound like forecasting
- fragile headline metrics that do not materially help answer the thesis question

### Practical Consequence for Chapters 5–8

When revising or drafting later chapters:

1. explain DTSE as a bounded evaluator before presenting any outputs
2. foreground transparent formulas and scenario logic over simulation spectacle
3. keep interpretation directional and conditional
4. trim any language that sounds like forecasting, ranking, or universal proof

### Final Rule

If a DTSE result is mathematically coherent but hard to defend verbally, it should be demoted before submission rather than rhetorically inflated.

That is the governing Option B principle.
