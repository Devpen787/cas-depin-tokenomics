## V2 Metric Tiering Policy

Status: synthesis control document  
Date captured: 2026-03-01  
Mode: `MODE: SYNTHESIS`

### Purpose

This document defines the Option B metric policy for V2.

It separates:
- core evaluative metrics that carry the thesis,
- bounded supporting metrics that add context when observable,
- optional or appendix-only metrics that are useful but too fragile to anchor the argument.

The aim is not to maximize the number of metrics. The aim is to keep the thesis transparent, defensible, and easy to explain under supervision or oral defense.

### Selection Principles

A metric belongs in Tier 1 only if most of the following are true:

1. It directly helps answer the thesis question.
2. Its formula or interpretation is clear enough to explain simply.
3. It can be defended without a long methodological apology.
4. It does not depend heavily on immature or noisy market conditions.
5. It works comparably across mechanism profiles, or its limits are easy to state.
6. It supports comparative diagnosis rather than false precision.

If a metric is useful but fails some of those tests, it drops to Tier 2.

If a metric is mainly contextual, highly fragile, or more business-report style than thesis-core evaluative logic, it drops to Tier 3.

### Tier 1: Core Evaluative Metrics

These metrics form the main thesis spine.

1. `Burn-to-Mint Ratio (BMR)`
2. `Net Emissions`
3. `Provider Retention`
4. `Provider Churn`
5. `Average Provider Profitability`
6. `Capacity Utilization`
7. `Demand Satisfaction`
8. `Volatility Proxy`
9. `First-Signal Timing / Failure-Signature Logic`

Why these stay central:
- they are formula-clear or interpretation-clear,
- they connect directly to provider retention, service continuity, and incentive-usage alignment,
- they are comparatively useful across mechanisms,
- they fit Option B's bounded-evaluator posture.

### Tier 2: Bounded Supporting Metrics

These metrics are useful, but should not become the thesis's main evidentiary backbone.

1. `Revenue per Node`
2. `Token Turnover / Speculative Velocity`
3. `FDV / Annualized Revenue`
4. `Active Node Count`
5. `Validated / Online Station Counts`
6. `Hardware Capacity`

Why these are bounded:
- they can be noisy in early-stage networks,
- they often depend on dated market conditions or incomplete revenue visibility,
- they are informative when carefully framed, but too fragile to carry the full argument.

### Tier 3: Context / Appendix / Optional Metrics

These metrics may be useful, but they should not be core to Option B unless exceptionally well supported.

1. `CAC vs Web2`
2. `Cost Advantage vs Centralized Incumbents`
3. `Protocol Take-Rate`
4. `SLA Adherence`
5. `Payback Period / Payback Ratio`
6. `Death Spiral Probability`

Why these are Tier 3:
- harder to observe consistently,
- easier to overclaim,
- more business-report or strategic-analysis style,
- greater defense burden than their thesis value usually justifies.

### Interpretation Rules

1. Tier 1 metrics should carry the main evaluative argument.
2. Tier 2 metrics may support context, caveated comparison, or appendix detail.
3. Tier 3 metrics should remain optional, appendix-bound, or clearly secondary.
4. No metric should be used as if it were more mature, more comparable, or more causal than the evidence allows.
5. Dated market metrics must always be treated as dated context, not timeless evidence.

### Thesis-Safe Framing

Use wording like:
- `primary evaluative metric`
- `bounded supporting metric`
- `dated contextual proxy`
- `not reliably observable`
- `used descriptively, not inferentially`

Avoid wording like:
- `proves robustness`
- `ranks real networks`
- `definitively measures sustainability`
- `predicts future network survival`

### Operating Consequence

This policy exists so later chapter drafting can be selective without losing clarity.

Option B does not remove metrics from the thesis because they are imperfect. It arranges them by:
- defensibility,
- transparency,
- observability,
- and usefulness to the actual research question.
