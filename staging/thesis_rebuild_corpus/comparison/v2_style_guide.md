## V2 Style Guide

Status: synthesis control document  
Date captured: 2026-02-28  
Mode: `MODE: SYNTHESIS`

### Purpose

This guide defines the V2 prose voice.

It exists so that:

1. Codex can enforce structural and evidentiary discipline without flattening the writing
2. GPT 5.2 can improve prose without drifting away from the thesis voice
3. Person A, B, and C material can be unified into one authored document

This is not a formatting guide. It is a writing guide.

### Voice Requirements

V2 prose should be:

1. academic
2. neutral
3. direct
4. readable
5. authored rather than assembled
6. intellectually selective

The reader should feel that the thesis was deliberately composed, not mechanically generated.
The prose should preserve visible judgment without drifting into opinionated or inflated writing.

### Tone

Preferred tone:

- analytical
- disciplined
- calm
- explanatory

Avoid:

- promotional language
- inflated confidence
- unnecessary drama
- consultant-speak
- defensive compliance prose

### Sentence-Level Preferences

Prefer:

1. variation in sentence length
2. one clear rhetorical move per sentence
3. concrete consequence before abstraction when possible
4. transitions that connect ideas, not just headings
5. verbs like:
   - `shows`
   - `indicates`
   - `suggests`
   - `reveals`
   - `registers`
   - `propagates`
   - `remains bounded by`
6. calibrated uncertainty where the evidence is limited
7. precise repetition of key terms where repetition improves clarity
8. occasional plainness when plain wording is more accurate than polished wording

Avoid overusing:

- `This thesis...`
- `The thesis...`
- `This section...`
- `The goal is...`
- `The scope is...`
- `It is important to note...`
- `It should be emphasized...`
- `In conclusion...`

### Paragraph Rules

1. One paragraph should do one job.
2. Paragraphs do not need to be the same size.
3. Complex concepts should not be compressed into a single dense block if readability suffers.
4. A paragraph should usually begin from a tension, claim, or observation, not from compliance framing.
5. Paragraph movement should be driven by thought, not by template symmetry.

### Preferred Writing Moves

Good V2 prose often does one of the following:

1. starts from a concrete DePIN tension
2. translates a mechanism into its operational consequence
3. clarifies how one subsystem affects another
4. connects a stress channel to a failure signature
5. states a limit without draining the paragraph of momentum
6. makes uncertainty visible without sounding evasive
7. sounds like the wording was chosen rather than auto-completed

### Banned Patterns

Avoid these recurring V1 habits:

1. compliance-first openings
2. over-symmetrical subsection rhythm
3. stacked abstractions with no concrete grip
4. sentence templates repeated across multiple paragraphs
5. contribution paragraphs that cram everything into one block
6. language that sounds like a polished summary instead of authored argument
7. bureaucratic list prose that uses `bold label + period + explanation` when normal prose or a cleaner list would teach better
8. generic “balanced” phrasing that sounds polished but says little

### Examples

#### Better

`A token holder can exit a position in seconds. An operator who has bought, installed, and calibrated site-specific hardware cannot.`

Why it works:

- starts from tension
- concrete immediately
- motivates the abstraction that follows

#### Worse

`Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks are blockchain-enabled systems that require a new evaluative framework.`

Why it fails:

- category definition before reader engagement
- abstract too early
- sounds generic

#### Better

`The results do not show that one mechanism class is universally superior. They show that different designs concentrate stress in different places and on different timelines.`

Why it works:

- clear
- bounded
- comparative
- interprets without overclaiming

#### Worse

`This thesis demonstrates that robustness depends on multiple factors and should therefore be evaluated carefully.`

Why it fails:

- vague
- generic
- thesis-machine phrasing

### Section Openings

Good section openings should usually do one of these:

1. state a real tension
2. identify a problem the reader can feel
3. show an operational consequence
4. ask the question the section is about to answer

Bad section openings usually:

1. define before motivating
2. announce structure before meaning
3. front-load cautionary language

### Questions and Major Analytical Turns

Research questions, contribution frames, and similar major turns should feel earned.

Prefer:

1. a short narrowing move before the question appears
2. enough lead-in that the reader understands why this is the question being asked
3. contribution language that follows the argument rather than interrupting it

Avoid:

1. dropping a question immediately after a heading with no rationale
2. presenting contributions before the problem has been narrowed
3. turning an important analytical move into a structural announcement

### Form Should Serve Comprehension

Use the form that helps the reader understand most clearly.

That may be:

1. one paragraph
2. two shorter paragraphs
3. a lead-in sentence plus a short list
4. a short numbered list

Do not choose a form only because it looks neat.
Do not over-edit away useful repetition or plainness if they improve understanding.

In particular, avoid list structures that sound like report-template prose, such as:

- `\textbf{Conceptual contribution.} ...`
- `\textbf{Methodological contribution.} ...`

unless the genre genuinely requires that structure and it improves comprehension.

### Evidence and Style Interaction

Good style does not weaken evidence discipline.

The rule is:

- make the sentence readable first
- then make the claim precise
- then make the evidence explicit

Do not use weak style as an excuse for overclaiming.
Do not use rigor as an excuse for lifeless prose.
Do not use tidy formatting as an excuse for less teachable writing.
Do not mistake polished wording for stronger judgment if the sentence becomes less precise.

### Model Use

#### Codex

Use for:

- structure
- consistency
- claim discipline
- terminology control
- final prose acceptance

#### GPT 5.2

Use for:

- transition options
- humanization
- sentence variation
- paragraph reshaping
- explanation density

Rule:

GPT 5.2 may improve the prose voice, but it must not be allowed to redefine the thesis argument, evidence hierarchy, or chapter contract.

### Final Standard

If a passage is:

- correct
- cited
- organized

but still sounds generic, mechanical, or over-compressed, it is not finished.

V2 prose should read like strong academic writing under control, not like a clean AI summary of the right points.
It should also sound chosen rather than auto-completed, and selective rather than generically well-balanced.
